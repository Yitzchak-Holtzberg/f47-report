// Long-Form Analysis
The Death of the Fighter
An essay on the oldest prophecy in military aviation — and why it keeps being wrong

E very generation of military planners has been convinced, at some point, that the fighter pilot's era was drawing to a close. The prophecy is older than the jet engine. After World War I, the great airpower theorist Giulio Douhet argued in The Command of the Air (1921) that strategic bombing would decide future wars — that fleets of bombers would render defensive fighters irrelevant because, as British Prime Minister Stanley Baldwin infamously declared in 1932, "the bomber will always get through." The Battle of Britain in 1940 proved him spectacularly wrong. Spitfires and Hurricanes — fighters — saved a nation.

The prophecy resurfaced in the 1950s, dressed in the language of the nuclear age. American strategists, entranced by the missile revolution and the advent of radar-guided air-to-air weapons, concluded that the era of turning, dogfighting aerial combat was simply over. Missiles would handle the threat at range. Supersonic speed would prevent close-in engagements. The gun was declared obsolete. This conviction was institutional, not merely theoretical — it was literally built into metal. The McDonnell F-4 Phantom II, the United States' premier fighter for the Vietnam era, entered service without an internal cannon. Fighters didn't need guns if they could kill at long range.

"The lack of an internal gun was the biggest mistake on the F-4. Bullets are cheap and tend to go where you aim them. I needed a gun, and I really wished I had one."

— CAPT. CHESIRE, F-4 PHANTOM PILOT, VIETNAM

Source: Wikipedia — McDonnell Douglas F-4 Phantom II

Vietnam and the Worst Kill Ratio in American History

The results over North Vietnam were catastrophic. By the end of the Rolling Thunder bombing campaign in 1968, the Phantom traded kills with MiGs at a nearly one-to-one ratio — the worst air combat performance in American history. The reasons were multiple and interlocking. The Phantom fell prey to the Pentagon doctrine that guns were obsolete and that air combat maneuvering was a dead art, made so by the advent of sophisticated new air-to-air missiles. But the AIM-7 Sparrow radar-guided missile achieved only around a 10% success rate in combat. Missiles had to fly a certain distance before they could arm. Electronic countermeasures, violent evasive action, and weather all degraded their performance. F-4 pilots sometimes found themselves in the uncomfortable situation of being too close to the MiGs to use their missiles, or having fired them without effect, only to find themselves well within the MiG's effective gun range.

The enemy aircraft — MiG-17s and MiG-21s — were slower than the Phantom but radically more maneuverable. They could out-turn the F-4 in a close-in fight. And crucially, in the early supersonic fighter era, American pilots were not trained in dogfighting tactics. Despite its value in the recent Korean War, dogfighting was not prioritized. The Phantom's design, optimized for nuclear-age long-range intercept, emphasized speed at the expense of agility — exactly what a close-in fight demanded. The institutional assumption that close combat would never happen had produced a fleet of aircraft and pilots that were unprepared for the war they actually found.

The response to Vietnam reshaped American military aviation for fifty years. The Navy established the Naval Fighter Weapons School — Top Gun — in 1969 specifically to restore dogfighting proficiency. The years after Vietnam witnessed a host of new technologies, new aircraft with new design philosophies, and new air combat schools such as Top Gun and the Air Force Aggressor Squadron, all emphasizing air-to-air combat. The period also witnessed the rise of the "fighter mafia" as former fighter pilots replaced the old Air Force brass. Out of this crucible came the F-15 Eagle and the F-16 Fighting Falcon — both aircraft with guns, both designed around maneuverability, both still flying front-line missions today.

Boyd's Ghost and the OODA Loop

No figure looms larger over the post-Vietnam reinvention of the fighter than Colonel John Boyd. A Korean War F-86 pilot who became the Air Force's foremost theoretician of air combat, Boyd developed two contributions that fundamentally changed how the military understood aerial fighting. The first was his Energy-Maneuverability theory, which provided a mathematical framework for understanding combat performance — thrust, drag, weight, and speed — and demonstrated precisely why the F-4 lost turning fights. The second was the OODA Loop: Observe, Orient, Decide, Act. Boyd accelerated his students' OODA loops, and the results were seen in improvements in the air war over North Vietnam.

Boyd's insight was that victory in combat belongs not necessarily to the fastest or the most lethal platform, but to the side that can cycle through the observe-orient-decide-act loop faster than its adversary. Disrupt your enemy's OODA loop — through speed, surprise, deception, or superior information — and they collapse into confusion. This framework explains why the missile-centric F-4 failed: its pilots were running a slow OODA loop, bound by rules of engagement requiring visual identification before firing, hampered by unreliable weapons, and stripped of the close-range option (the gun) that would let them act when the loop finally closed. Boyd's Aerial Attack Study showed that an agile fighter could out-maneuver missiles. The technology had outpaced the tactics — until the tactics caught up and rewrote the technology.

Musk at the Podium: "The Fighter Jet Era Has Passed"

History repeated itself with remarkable fidelity at the Air Force Association's Air Warfare Symposium in February 2020. In a room packed full of U.S. Air Force personnel, SpaceX founder Elon Musk issued a bold pronouncement: "The fighter jet era has passed. Locally autonomous drone warfare is where it's at, where the future will be." The reaction in the room was immediate — a collective pause. A decade of drone warfare in Iraq, Afghanistan, and Syria had fueled a growing conviction, loudest in the tech sector, that piloted fighters were becoming expensive anachronisms. Why spend $150 million on an F-22, the argument went, when a drone could do the same job at a fraction of the cost and zero pilot risk?

Musk returned to the theme in late 2024, posting alongside a video of synchronized Chinese drones: "Meanwhile, some idiots are still building manned fighters like the F-35." He argued that crewed fighter jets are just "an inefficient way to extend the range of missiles and bombs" and that it is "laughably easy" to take down fighter jets, including stealth ones, citing the war in Ukraine as proof. The comments drew support from political allies including Matt Gaetz, who called for abandoning the F-35 entirely in favor of drones. The argument had popular appeal, genuine technological grounding, and very serious implications for the $2+ trillion F-35 program. It also had a powerful historical echo: it was, structurally, the same argument made about the gun in 1955.

"Elon's hot take about the F-35 being replaced by drones is because he understands technology, but he doesn't understand tactics."

— USAF SERGEANT, quoted in 19FortyFive (2024)

Why the Drone Maximalists Are (Probably) Wrong — For Now

The drone maximalist case has real substance and should not be dismissed. But it has a critical flaw: it confuses the drones that exist today with the drones that would need to exist to replace a fifth-generation fighter. The small drones shown in viral swarm videos are utterly incapable of performing the sorts of missions the F-35 was designed to fly. More fundamentally, the AI that would be required for combat aircraft to truly replace human pilots struggles with rapid OODA loops, electronic warfare, and complex, ambiguous kill chains. Being a fighter pilot requires making split-second decisions repeatedly, sometimes without complete information, and always with a glut of related data pouring in from sensors. This sort of creative problem-solving is outside the realm of what AI can reliably perform.

Today's most advanced AI systems for combat aircraft still rely on human intervention in several ways. Specific AI agents can perform specific tasks, like air-to-air combat, but can't perform others, like air-to-ground operations or even landing. And until these systems are robust and trustworthy enough to operate on their own, you have to manage concerns about jamming and other means of electronic warfare that could interfere with autonomous systems in a combat zone. Brig. Gen. Doug Wickert, the USAF commander overseeing AI piloting technology development, put the timeline in perspective: "There may be someday we can completely rely on robotized warfare, but it is centuries away." That is not a dismissal of the drone revolution — it is a precise diagnosis of where the technology actually sits.

There is also a strategic asymmetry argument that drone advocates often ignore. Every major military is working to develop countermeasures against individual drones and swarms. These militaries are observing drones in action in Ukraine and using those observations to develop systems to defend ships and bases. The cheap FPV drones that have been devastatingly effective against Russian armor in Ukraine are operating in a permissive electromagnetic environment without serious enemy air defenses. A Chinese J-20 or the hypothetical successor to the S-400 system represents a categorically different threat than a Russian T-72 in a field. The drone that dominates a low-tech conflict may be trivially defeated in a high-end peer competition. Countermeasures are being developed so fast that cheap drones may soon be obsolete for many of the missions they are performing today.

The F-47 as Paradox: The Last Fighter and the First of Something Else

Which brings us to the F-47, and the peculiar position it occupies in this debate. The program is, simultaneously, the strongest argument that manned fighters still matter and the clearest acknowledgment that the pure manned-fighter era may indeed be ending. The Air Force is spending over $20 billion to build what could be — by some analysts' reckoning — the last manned air superiority fighter the United States will ever procure. And it is doing so by baking the drone revolution directly into the aircraft's DNA: the F-47's defining feature is not its stealth or its range or its adaptive engines, but its ability to command fleets of autonomous CCA wingmen. The manned fighter survives, but only by becoming something more than a fighter.

Rather than crewed aircraft being replaced completely by unmanned systems, we are seeing a partnership of piloted and autonomous systems, with unmanned aircraft having various levels of trusted autonomy depending on the operational and tactical circumstances. The F-47 is the physical embodiment of this partnership — a human judgment node at the center of a distributed machine swarm. The pilot is not there to pull g's and dogfight anymore; the pilot is there to run Boyd's OODA loop at the highest level of the battlespace, to provide the contextual decision-making and ethical judgment that no current AI can reliably replicate, and to authorize lethal force in the complex, ambiguous environment of a peer conflict that a drone following algorithmic rules simply cannot navigate.

And yet the F-47 does not fully escape the historical trap. Its tailless configuration trades the F-22's extraordinary close-in agility for bomber-class stealth and dramatically extended range. Its designers are betting, as the Pentagon bet in the 1950s, that if you can see far enough and shoot far enough, you will never need to turn and fight. The AIM-260's reported 100+ nautical mile range makes the argument more compelling than the Sparrow's 10% hit rate ever did. But history has a way of finding the gap between doctrine and reality. Rules of engagement can force visual identification. Electronic warfare can blind long-range sensors. Communications can be jammed, severing the link between the F-47 and its drone swarm. And in that moment — the moment that the technology fails and the geometry closes — the question of whether the F-47 can fight its way out may become very real.

"Despite impressive gains in autonomous technology, manned fighter aircraft will continue to provide the underpinnings of the air superiority mission for decades into the future. Air superiority — the mission that fighter aircraft execute — is literally one of the most important combat capabilities a nation can possess."

— Defense News, March 2020

The Verdict

The fighter is not dead. But it is metamorphosing into something its designers of 1944 or 1965 or even 1997 would struggle to recognize. The F-47 is not, at its core, a faster F-22. It is a networked command platform that happens to be capable of going Mach 2 and evading radar. The pilot inside it is not primarily a stick-and-rudder artist — he or she is a battlefield orchestrator, a human decision node in a lattice of autonomous systems. This represents not the end of the fighter tradition, but its highest evolution: the most capable crewed combat aircraft in history, flying alongside machines that do not fear death, do not tire, and do not flinch.

Whether the F-47 is the last of its kind — the final manned air superiority fighter before full autonomy renders the cockpit a historical artifact — remains genuinely unknown. The generals commissioning it today might privately believe it is. But the same could have been said of the F-4 in 1960, the F-15 in 1975, or the F-22 in 2000. Each time the fighter was declared obsolete, it came back stronger. Each time technology promised to replace the pilot, the battlefield found new ways to make the human irreplaceable. Boyd's ghost hovers over the F-47 program with a single, pointed question: what happens when your missiles miss, your drones go dark, and a MiG is at your six — do you have a gun?
